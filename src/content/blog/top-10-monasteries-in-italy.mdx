Italy is where Western monasticism was codified — St. Benedict wrote his Rule
here. Its monasteries range from dramatic clifftop abbeys to working
communities that still chant the offices daily. These ten are essential.

## 1. Monte Cassino, Lazio

The mother house of the Benedictine order, founded by St. Benedict around 529,
rebuilt after wartime destruction — the symbolic heart of the tradition.

## 2. Sacra di San Michele, Piedmont

A medieval abbey on a rocky summit above the Susa Valley, the inspiration for
*The Name of the Rose* and a Piedmont landmark.

## 3. Subiaco — Sacro Speced, Lazio

The monastery built around the cave where St. Benedict first lived as a hermit,
its walls covered in early frescoes.

## 4. Monastery of San Marco, Florence

Where Fra Angelico frescoed the friars' cells one by one — a Renaissance
masterpiece that is also a Dominican house.

## 5. Certosa di Pavia, Lombardy

A breathtakingly ornate Carthusian charterhouse, one of the most decorated
monastic buildings in the world.

## 6. Abbey of Montecassino's rival in beauty: Sant'Antimo, Tuscany

A serene Romanesque abbey in the Val d'Orcia where the liturgy is still sung.

## 7. Camaldoli, Tuscany

The hermitage and monastery in the Casentino forest, home of the Camaldolese
since the 11th century — a place built for silence.

## 8. La Verna, Tuscany

The mountain sanctuary where St. Francis received the stigmata, a Franciscan
pilgrimage site in deep forest.

## 9. Abbazia di Novacella, South Tyrol

A working Augustinian monastery with a famed library and one of the world's
oldest monastic wineries.

## 10. San Giorgio Maggiore, Venice

A Benedictine monastery on its own island, its Palladian church facing the
lagoon — monasticism and high architecture combined.

## Visiting living monasteries

Many of these are active communities, not just monuments. Dress modestly
(covered shoulders and knees), keep silence in churches and cloisters, switch
phones off, and respect areas closed to the public — the enclosure is the
monks' home. Attending a sung office (Vespers especially) is often the most
moving way to experience them, but as a guest, not a spectator.

## Practicalities

Some monasteries offer guesthouse stays (*foresteria*) for those seeking
retreat — book directly and well ahead. Opening hours bend around the liturgy
and midday closure; check before travelling. Several sell monastic produce —
wine, honey, herbal liqueurs — that funds the community.
