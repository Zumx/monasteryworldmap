Some monasteries are so extraordinary in setting or significance that they
became global icons. Each represents a peak of what monastic communities built
in pursuit of withdrawal from the world.

## Meteora, Greece

Orthodox monasteries perched on towering rock pillars in Thessaly — built to be
unreachable, now among the most breathtaking sights in Europe.

## Mont-Saint-Michel, France

A Benedictine abbey crowning a tidal island, one of the most recognised
silhouettes on Earth and a millennium-old pilgrimage site.

## Potala Palace, Tibet

The vast hilltop monastery-palace of the Dalai Lamas in Lhasa — the symbolic
heart of Tibetan Buddhism.

## Saint Catherine's, Egypt

At the foot of Mount Sinai, one of the oldest continuously inhabited Christian
monasteries in the world, holding an unmatched ancient library.

## Mount Athos, Greece

A self-governing monastic peninsula of twenty Orthodox monasteries — a thousand
years of unbroken contemplative life, access tightly restricted.

## Taktsang (Tiger's Nest), Bhutan

A monastery clinging to a Himalayan cliff face above Paro — perhaps the most
photographed Buddhist site in the world.

## Monte Cassino, Italy

The cradle of Western monasticism, founded by St. Benedict, rebuilt after total
wartime destruction.

## Sumela, Turkey

A Greek Orthodox monastery built into a sheer cliff face in the Pontic
Mountains — a frescoed eyrie above a forest gorge.

## Rila Monastery, Bulgaria

The largest Eastern Orthodox monastery in Bulgaria, a UNESCO site of striped
arcades and vivid frescoes in a mountain valley.

## Ganden & the great Gelug monasteries, Tibet

Vast monastic universities that once housed thousands of monks — institutions
of learning as much as devotion.

## Why these became icons

Each pushed an idea to its limit: inaccessibility (Meteora, Taktsang),
endurance (Saint Catherine's, Mount Athos), or scale of devotion (Potala,
Ganden). They are famous because they show how far communities went to build a
place set apart from the world.

## Visiting the icons

The accessible ones (Meteora, Mont-Saint-Michel, Rila) are heavily visited and
tightly governed — strict dress codes, timed access, and active worship in
progress. Others (Mount Athos, parts of Tibet and Bhutan) require permits,
restrict numbers or gender, and book far ahead. Plan around their rules; the
restriction is part of what preserved them.
