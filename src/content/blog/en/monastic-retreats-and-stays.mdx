The guesthouse wing is among the oldest features of Western monasticism. Chapter four of the Rule of St Benedict states that the monastery ought to receive guests as if receiving Christ himself. Practical hospitality has followed from that instruction for fifteen centuries: a clean room, a simple meal, and an invitation to participate in the rhythm of the monastic day as much or as little as a visitor chooses. The modern version of this arrangement ranges from a spare dormitory bed at a pilgrimage monastery to a comfortable private room at an abbey hotel operating at the level of a four-star guesthouse — and several points in between.

## Italian Benedictine Ospitalità

The Italian Benedictine tradition of ospitalità monastica is the most developed in the world for non-religious visitors. Subiaco, the complex of monasteries in the hills east of Rome where St Benedict himself lived as a hermit before founding Monte Cassino, receives visitors in its guesthouse throughout the year. The Sacro Speco ("Holy Cave") and the larger Monastery of St Scholastica are both active communities; overnight guests follow a modified version of the monastic schedule, attending Vespers and Compline and eating in a guest refectory. The setting — tiered stone buildings built directly into a cliff face above a river gorge — is exceptional, and the guesthouses are not expensive.

Monte Cassino, the mother house of the Benedictine order founded in 529 and rebuilt after its destruction in the Second World War, operates a guesthouse for pilgrims and guests. The rebuilt abbey is architecturally austere but the site carries enormous historical weight, and the abbey museum contains remarkable manuscripts and artworks recovered from the pre-war collection.

Camaldoli, in the Casentino forest in Tuscany, is a Camaldolese community — a Benedictine reform movement that combines cenobitic and hermit life. The lower monastery (the foresteria) takes paying guests in comfortable rooms; a forest walk of about half an hour leads up to the hermitage proper, a compound of separate stone hermitages where the monks live in stricter enclosure. The foresteria meals are good — the community runs a pharmacy and shop selling honey, liqueurs, and herbal preparations from its own production.

## French Cistercian Stays

Abbaye Notre-Dame de Tamié, in the French Alps above the Tarentaise valley, is a Cistercian community that accepts guests in its hôtellerie for stays of one to eight nights. The cheese made here — Tamié — is sold in the abbey shop and through a few specialist retailers. Guests eat in the guest refectory, attend services if they choose, and have access to the surrounding mountain landscape. The abbey's setting above a high valley is one of the finest of any working monastery open to guests.

Abbaye de Lérins, on the Île Saint-Honorat off Cannes — accessible by ferry from the mainland — is a Cistercian community that operates a guest programme primarily for retreat participants. The island is small and the monastery occupies much of it; the combination of sea views, ancient architecture, and strict silence creates an atmosphere difficult to replicate anywhere else in France. The community also produces wine from vineyards on the island.

## The Trappist Retreat at Sept-Fons

The Abbaye Notre-Dame de Sept-Fons in the Allier department of central France is an OCSO (Trappist) community that maintains a strong silence culture and receives guests who want to participate genuinely in monastic life rather than simply stay in a scenic building. The guest schedule follows the full office — Vigils at 3:25 in the morning, seven further offices through the day, work periods, and lectio divina. This is not a spa retreat; it is an opportunity to live the monastic day alongside a community doing so permanently. Prior correspondence and a brief application are required.

## Stift Heiligenkreuz

Heiligenkreuz Abbey in the Vienna Woods, founded by Margrave Leopold III in 1133, is the oldest continuously inhabited Cistercian monastery in the world. It operates a guesthouse (the Gästehaus) and runs retreat programmes throughout the year. The abbey's Gregorian chant programme — the community released a commercially successful chant recording in 2008 — is central to its identity, and guests can attend all the canonical hours. The abbey is accessible from Vienna by train and local bus, making it one of the most convenient monastery stays in Central Europe.

## Anglican Mirfield

The Community of the Resurrection at Mirfield, West Yorkshire, is an Anglican religious community that runs an extensive guest programme. The house of retreat accepts visitors for private retreats; the guesthouse is open year-round. Mirfield operates differently from Catholic or Orthodox houses — the silence rule is less total, visitors are not expected to attend the full office, and the atmosphere is closer to that of a theological conference centre than a strict enclosure. For visitors from Protestant or Anglican backgrounds who find full Roman or Orthodox observance unfamiliar, Mirfield provides a gentler entry to the experience.

## Plum Village

Plum Village, founded by the Vietnamese Zen master Thich Nhat Hanh near Bordeaux in 1982, is the largest Buddhist monastery in the Western world. It operates across several hamlets and accepts guests for scheduled retreats throughout most of the year. The practice tradition is engaged Buddhism — mindfulness in daily activities, walking meditation, dharma talks, and periods of shared silence — rather than the intensive sitting retreats associated with Japanese or Korean Zen. The community has become particularly well-known for its work with young people and families, and retreats designed for different groups are scheduled throughout the summer season.

## Koyasan Shukubo

Mount Koya (Koyasan) in Wakayama Prefecture, Japan, is the headquarters of Shingon Buddhism and home to over a hundred temples, approximately fifty of which operate shukubo — temple lodging for overnight guests. The stays typically include a vegetarian multi-course dinner (shojin ryori), a tatami sleeping room, and the opportunity to attend early morning meditation and fire ritual (goma). Guests are not expected to participate beyond their own comfort; the morning ceremony is open to spectators. The forest cemetery of Okunoin, extending for two kilometres beneath ancient cryptomeria trees, is best visited at dusk or before dawn when the lanterns that line the path are lit.

## What to Pack and What to Expect

For Western monastic stays: modest, dark-coloured clothing; a habit of waking early; ear plugs if you are a light sleeper in a stone building; and patience with simple food. Most guesthouses provide towels and basic toiletries but not the amenities of a hotel. Electronic devices are generally permitted in guest rooms but should not be used in common areas, cloisters, or churches.

For Buddhist temple stays: loose, comfortable clothing in neutral colours; footwear that slips off easily (you will remove shoes many times a day); and an open attitude toward unfamiliar ritual.

Donations versus flat rates vary considerably. Many contemplative monasteries in Europe operate on a suggested donation (typically EUR 40–70 per night including meals), while others — particularly those operating purpose-built guesthouses — charge fixed rates. In either case, the price is well below equivalent hotel accommodation, and the surplus benefits the community directly.
