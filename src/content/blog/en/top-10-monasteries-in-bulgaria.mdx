Bulgarian monasticism has a dual history of cultural preservation and political resistance. During the five centuries of Ottoman rule (1396–1878), the monasteries functioned as the primary institutions maintaining Bulgarian language, literacy, and Orthodox Christian identity at a time when all other Bulgarian institutions had been eliminated. Monks copied manuscripts, ran schools, and sheltered rebels during the National Revival movement of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Under communism (1944–1989), monasteries were classified as cultural monuments rather than functioning religious communities, deprived of income, and staffed by a minimum of monks to maintain appearances. The revival since 1989 has been significant — monastic populations have grown, buildings have been restored, and the Bulgarian Orthodox Church has reclaimed its central social role.

## 1. Rila Monastery, Kyustendil

The largest and most famous monastery in Bulgaria, Rila is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the spiritual heart of Bulgarian Orthodoxy. Founded in the tenth century by St John of Rila, a hermit who became the country's patron saint, the current complex dates primarily from the nineteenth century after a devastating fire. The multi-storey arcaded courtyard with its black-and-white zebra arches and the Nativity Church (1834–1837) with its frescoes covering every interior surface represent the peak of the Bulgarian National Revival architectural style. Rila is active (approximately twenty monks), receives over a million visitors a year, and operates a guesthouse for those wishing to stay overnight. The monastery museum holds the remarkable Raphael's Cross, a miniature altar cross carved with 140 biblical scenes and 1,500 human figures by the monk Raphael, who spent twelve years on it and subsequently went blind.

## 2. Bachkovo Monastery, Plovdiv

The second largest Orthodox monastery in Bulgaria, Bachkovo was founded in 1083 by the Georgian military commander Grigorios Bakuriani in the Rhodope Mountains south of Plovdiv. The main church of the Dormition of the Virgin holds a venerated icon of the Virgin that attracts pilgrims throughout the year. The monastery survived Ottoman rule intact and contains frescoes spanning from the eleventh to nineteenth centuries, making its walls a compressed survey of Bulgarian and Byzantinepainting. The ossuary church of the Archangels (a separate building outside the main walls) contains some of the best-preserved medieval frescoes in Bulgaria.

## 3. Troyan Monastery, Lovech

Troyan, in the central Balkan range, is the third largest monastery in Bulgaria and one of the most active as a pilgrimage destination for the icon of the Three-Handed Virgin (Trojerucica), a copy of the famous icon from Hilandar on Mount Athos. The monastery played a significant role in the April 1876 uprising against Ottoman rule; the revolutionary leader Vasil Levski was briefly sheltered here and the monastery suffered reprisals afterward. The current church dates from 1835 with frescoes by the celebrated Bulgarian painter Zahari Zograf.

## 4. Preobrazhenski Monastery, Tarnovo

The Monastery of the Transfiguration is built against a cliff face in the gorge of the Yantra river just north of Veliko Tarnovo, the medieval capital of the Second Bulgarian Empire. Founded in the fourteenth century, it was restored in the nineteenth and contains significant frescoes by Zahari Zograf in the main church (1851). The cliff setting — with buildings rising directly from the rock above the river gorge — is among the most dramatic in Bulgaria, and the monastery is accessible on foot from Tarnovo by a gorge trail.

## 5. Rozhen Monastery, Blagoevgrad

Rozhen (the Nativity of the Virgin) sits in the hills above the town of Melnik in the Struma valley near the Greek border — a region of sandstone pyramids and Bulgaria's finest wine. Founded in the twelfth century and expanded in the sixteenth, Rozhen is notable for its well-preserved Renaissance-period katholikon and for its defensive tower, rare among Bulgarian monasteries. The combination of monastic architecture, surrounding vineyards, and the otherworldly sandstone landscape makes Rozhen one of the most photographed monasteries in the country.

## 6. Aladzha Monastery, Varna

Aladzha is a medieval rock-cut monastery carved into the limestone cliffs above the Black Sea coast near Varna, in the Zlatni Pyasatsi Natural Park. The complex, active from the twelfth to the seventeenth centuries, consists of two levels of cells, chapels, and spaces cut from the cliff face. No monks have lived here since the Ottoman period, and it operates today as an archaeological museum, but the site retains traces of frescoes in the upper chapel and conveys the ascetic ambition of its founders effectively. The name derives from a Turkish word meaning "multicoloured," referring to the surviving painted surfaces.

## 7. Glozhene Monastery, Lovech

Glozhene (St George) occupies a clifftop position above the Iskar Gorge in central Bulgaria, reached by a steep road or a forest path. Founded in the thirteenth century and damaged in the Ottoman period, the current church dates from 1858. The monastery is active under the Lovech Eparchy and receives visitors daily. The gorge setting, with the Iskar river visible far below, is striking, and the monastery is a natural stopping point for travellers following the Iskar Gorge route between Sofia and Pleven.

## 8. Dryanovo Monastery, Gabrovo

Dryanovo (St Archangel Michael) in the Dryanovo Gorge near Gabrovo was the site of an important episode in the April 1876 uprising: a band of revolutionaries led by Pop Harion Draganov and Tsanko Dyustabanov held off a vastly larger Ottoman force here for nine days before the monastery was taken and burned. The current buildings date from after the uprising; the site includes a museum dedicated to the battle and the graves of the defenders. The monastery is active and combines historical significance with a scenic gorge setting.

## 9. Sokolski Monastery, Gabrovo

The Assumption Monastery of Sokolski (or the Sokolski Monastery of the Assumption of the Holy Virgin), founded in 1833, is the youngest major monastery on this list and one of the most architecturally coherent examples of the National Revival style. Its founder, Father Yosif Sokolski, later became a controversial figure when he converted to Catholicism and briefly became head of the Bulgarian Uniate Church before being abducted to Russia. The monastery is active under the Gabrovo Eparchy.

## 10. Cherepish Monastery, Vratsa

Cherepish (the Assumption of the Virgin) is situated in the Iskar Gorge, the same dramatic canyon that shelters Glozhene but further downstream in the Vratsa region. Founded in the fourteenth century, destroyed and rebuilt multiple times, the current complex dates from the nineteenth century. The monastery is active and operates a small guesthouse; it is accessible from Sofia by road or by the Sofia–Vidin railway line, which passes through the gorge.

## Visiting Bulgarian Monasteries

Most Bulgarian monasteries are open daily from early morning to late afternoon. Admission is free though donations are expected; some monasteries charge a small fee for museums or photography. Dress modestly — shoulders and knees covered — and women should have a headscarf available for Orthodox churches.
