Most of the world's great monasteries are still inhabited by living communities. The monks or nuns did not build their cloister to be a tourist attraction, and the fact that visitors are welcome — or at least tolerated — is a genuine act of hospitality that can be withdrawn if abused. Knowing how to behave in a monastery is partly a matter of courtesy and partly a matter of practicality: communities that feel respected by their visitors are more open, more generous with access, and more likely to maintain the welcome for those who follow. The following guidance covers the main categories of visitor behaviour that matter across different monastic traditions.

## Dress Codes

The most universal monastery rule concerns dress. In every tradition, from Benedictine to Theravada Buddhist, a place of worship is a place of dignity, and visitors who dress as if they are at a beach or a sports event signal either ignorance or indifference. The minimum standard in Western Christian, Eastern Christian, and Islamic-adjacent contexts is covered shoulders and covered knees for all visitors. In Orthodox contexts, this minimum is extended further.

At Orthodox monasteries — Greek, Romanian, Russian, Serbian, Bulgarian — women are expected to wear a skirt that reaches below the knee (not trousers, even loose ones) and to cover their hair with a scarf or headscarf inside the church and often throughout the monastic grounds. Some Orthodox monasteries provide scarves or wraparound skirts at the entrance for unprepared visitors; others do not, and unprepared visitors may simply be refused entry. At many Romanian monasteries, the entrance has a box of skirts and scarves on a donation basis. Men should wear trousers rather than shorts. Sleeveless tops are not acceptable for either gender.

Mount Athos applies the most extreme dress code combined with the most extreme access restriction. Women are excluded entirely — the abaton has been in force without exception since 1060. Men visiting the Holy Mountain should wear long trousers, closed shoes (not sandals), and bring a simple dark top for monastery visits. No shorts, no vests, no athletic wear.

At Buddhist monasteries, the emphasis is on respect for sacred objects and spaces. Footwear is removed before entering the main hall and often the entire temple precincts in Southeast Asian Theravada traditions. This applies universally — visitors who cannot remove shoes for medical reasons should contact the monastery in advance. Shoulders and knees should be covered; many Thai wats provide sarongs at the entrance for a small fee. At Japanese Zen and Shingon temples, the footwear convention applies inside main halls but not always outdoors; follow signs and the example of other visitors.

## Silence in Cloisters

The cloister — the covered walkway around the central courtyard of a Western monastery — is typically a zone of silence and contemplation. Speaking above a quiet murmur is inappropriate, and loud conversation or phone calls are disruptive. This applies whether the cloister is actively used by monks or has become a visitor walkway in a museum monastery. The cloisters of Fontenay, Fontevraud, or the Spanish Cistercian monasteries still carry the quality of silence they were built for, and visitors who enter that atmosphere with noise remove something from the experience for everyone.

In Zen Buddhist monasteries, the meditation hall (zendo) is always a silence zone. Photography and speech are prohibited, and entry is often restricted to practising members. At Eihei-ji and similar training monasteries, visitors walk through the complex on designated paths and are not permitted to enter working areas. The movements of training monks on the covered walkways of Eihei-ji are part of the practice; visitors who photograph or otherwise disrupt these movements are interfering with someone's religious life.

## Photography Rules

Photography rules vary widely and must be understood rather than assumed. The general principle is: always ask before photographing in a church or temple, and accept no as an answer without argument.

Many Orthodox churches prohibit photography entirely inside the building, regardless of flash, monopod, or other variables. The prohibition is theological as well as practical: the iconostasis and the icons it displays are liturgical objects, not decorative artworks, and photographing them for social media is considered a form of desecration by many communities. Where photography is permitted inside Orthodox churches, flash is universally prohibited — the damage to aged tempera icon surfaces from repeated flash is cumulative and irreversible.

Japanese temples vary significantly. Most allow photography in gardens and outdoors; many prohibit it inside the main hall, especially near the principal image. Some allow photography but prohibit tripods, selfie sticks, or any device that involves extended presence near the altar. Signs in Japanese and English usually make the rules clear.

Monastic guesthouses and living quarters are never appropriate for photography without explicit invitation. Photographing monks or nuns in their daily activities — eating, working, processing to services — requires either official permission or a clear invitation from the subjects. The instinct to document a colourful scene can easily shade into treating a religious community as performers for one's camera roll.

## Services and Liturgy

Visitors may attend liturgical services in most active monasteries unless specifically excluded. The etiquette varies by tradition.

At Orthodox services, standing is the norm — pews are rare in Orthodox churches, and most services involve standing for extended periods. Seating may be available along the walls for the elderly or infirm. Visitors should not sit on the sanctuary steps or lean against the iconostasis. The sign of the cross in the Orthodox tradition goes right-to-left (not left-to-right as in the Western tradition), and visitors need not attempt it, but should not make the Western sign in a way that suggests they are consciously substituting it.

At Catholic Benedictine or Cistercian services, visitors may sit in the nave or side aisles. Sitting during the Gospel reading is disrespectful; standing when the community stands is courteous. Non-Catholics should not receive communion. At monastic offices (Matins, Lauds, Vespers, Compline) which are shorter and less eucharistic than Mass, participation by sitting quietly and following the chant is entirely appropriate.

At Buddhist services, visitors should not walk in front of the altar or approach the main image closely during a ceremony. Sitting cross-legged on the floor (not in a chair above the monks if seating is provided) is appropriate; pointing the soles of the feet toward the altar or toward senior monks is considered disrespectful in most Asian Buddhist cultures.

## Donations and Museum Monasteries

There is a practical and ethical difference between an active monastery that receives visitors and a museum monastery that was once a monastic community. At an active monastery, a donation (whether formalised as an entrance fee or offered in a box) contributes directly to the community's upkeep and supports a living religious life. At a museum monastery — Fontenay, Cluny, Santes Creus, many English abbeys — the entrance fee funds cultural heritage management.

Both matter, but the relationship is different. At a museum monastery, the appropriate frame is archaeological and architectural. At an active monastery, you are a guest in someone's home, and the donation is a form of thanks for hospitality.

## The Difference That Matters

The single most important thing a visitor can bring to a monastery is attentiveness — the willingness to be quiet enough to perceive what the place is, rather than moving through it quickly with a camera and leaving. The schedules, silences, and rules of monastery life were not designed for tourism; they were designed to create conditions in which prayer is possible. Visitors who approach those conditions with respect, even without sharing the faith they express, generally find something worth having.
