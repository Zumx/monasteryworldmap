Greece is exceptional among European countries for the continuity and density of its monastic tradition. The Byzantine state actively supported monastery-building from the fourth century onward, and the Ottoman period, while limiting in many respects, allowed the Orthodox Church to maintain its institutional life in ways that Balkan Christianity could not have survived without. The result is a landscape studded with monasteries that range from dramatically cliff-perched complexes to quiet island communities, from the active all-male republic of Mount Athos to accessible day-trip destinations for anyone travelling in the country.

## 1. Great Meteoron, Thessaly

The largest and oldest of the six surviving Meteora monasteries, Great Meteoron (the Monastery of the Transfiguration) occupies the highest pillar in the Meteora complex near Kalambaka in central Greece. Founded in the fourteenth century by St Athanasios of Meteora, it was expanded by the Serbian king Stefan Uroš IV Dušan's donation and reached its architectural peak in the sixteenth century under the abbot Nektarios. The frescoes in the catholicon include graphic scenes of the persecution of early Christians that are among the most vivid in Greek monastic painting. Opening hours are Wednesday through Monday, approximately 9:00–17:00; closed Tuesday.

## 2. Varlaam Monastery, Meteora

Founded in 1541 by the brothers Nektarios and Theophanes Apsaras, Varlaam is the second largest Meteora monastery and widely considered the finest architecturally. Its catholicon of All Saints preserves the original sixteenth-century frescoes in exceptional condition. The net-and-winch system that was the only method of ascent before the stairs were cut in the 1920s is still visible; a large barrel used for hauling supplies up the cliff remains in the reception tower. Open Saturday through Thursday.

## 3. Roussanou Monastery, Meteora

Roussanou, a women's community since the 1980s, is the most vertiginously positioned of the Meteora monasteries — its entire base appears to merge with the rock column on which it sits, with the cliff face falling away on three sides. Founded in the sixteenth century, the monastery was abandoned for over a century before being re-occupied. The catholicon frescoes (1560) include an unusual Apocalypse cycle. Open daily except Tuesday.

## 4. St Stephen's Monastery, Meteora

The most accessible of the six Meteora monasteries (reached by a bridge rather than steps), St Stephen's is a women's community that operates a museum of notable quality alongside the active monastic programme. The older catholicon contains good sixteenth-century frescoes; the larger eighteenth-century church is the main worship space today. Open Tuesday through Sunday.

## 5. Holy Trinity Monastery, Meteora

Holy Trinity (Agia Triada) appears in the 1981 James Bond film For Your Eyes Only, which brought it international attention it had neither sought nor particularly benefited from. The monastery dates from the fifteenth century and is reached by 140 steps cut into the rock. The catholicon frescoes (1741) are in reasonable condition; the site is active with a small male community. The isolated position — reached by a longer path than most of the other monasteries — keeps visitor numbers lower. Open Monday, Wednesday, and Friday through Sunday.

## 6. St Nicholas Anapafsas, Meteora

The smallest of the six accessible Meteora monasteries, St Nicholas Anapafsas (the "monastery of rest") is notable primarily for its frescoes by Theophanes the Cretan (Theophanes Strelitzas), one of the most celebrated icon painters of the sixteenth century, painted in 1527. The vertical compression of the pillar forced the architect to stack the functional spaces — narthex, nave, catholicon — in floors rather than spreading them horizontally. Open Monday and Wednesday through Sunday.

## 7. Monastery of St John the Theologian, Patmos

The monastery on the island of Patmos, founded in 1088 by the monk Christodoulos with imperial support, occupies the summit of the island's main town and is visible from the sea as the defining silhouette of the hillside. St John the Theologian wrote the Book of Revelation in a cave below the monastery (the Cave of the Apocalypse, also a UNESCO site). The monastery complex is a UNESCO World Heritage Site; its treasury of manuscripts, icons, vestments, and imperial documents represents one of the finest collections of Byzantine material culture outside Constantinople. Open mornings; the treasury has restricted hours.

## 8. Hosios Loukas, Boeotia

Hosios Loukas, near Distomo in Boeotia, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the finest surviving example of middle Byzantine monastic architecture. The Catholicon of St Luke (the holy hermit, not the evangelist) and the adjacent Theotokos church, built in the tenth and eleventh centuries, are covered internally with mosaics of the highest quality, including a famous Christ Pantocrator in the dome. The crypt beneath the catholicon preserves painted decoration of the earlier period; the contrast between the luminous upper mosaic programme and the intimate painted lower level is one of the great spatial experiences in Byzantine architecture. The site is still active under the Metropolis of Thebes and receives visitors daily; the combination with a detour to Delphi makes a full day from Athens.

## 9. Nea Moni, Chios

Nea Moni ("New Monastery") on the island of Chios is a UNESCO World Heritage Site founded by Byzantine Emperor Constantine IX Monomachos in 1042. The catholicon's mosaics — produced by craftsmen from Constantinople — are among the finest surviving examples of eleventh-century Byzantine mosaic art. The monastery was sacked by Ottoman forces in 1822 during the Chios Massacre; the ossuary contains the skulls and bones of the victims arranged in glass cases, a sombre addition to the artistic glory of the site. A small community of nuns maintains the monastery.

## 10. Daphni Monastery, Attica

Daphni, on the outskirts of Athens near the ancient Sacred Way to Eleusis, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site containing some of the finest Byzantine mosaics in the world. The eleventh-century catholicon mosaics — particularly the Christ Pantocrator in the dome — represent Byzantine art at a high point of theological confidence and technical mastery. The monastery was occupied by Cistercian monks from Western Europe during the Frankish period (thirteenth to fifteenth centuries) and then returned to Orthodox use; this unusual history is visible in the Gothic pointed arches added to the Byzantine structure. The site has been under extended restoration and access has been intermittent; check current opening status before visiting.

## Planning a Monastery Tour in Greece

The Meteora monasteries are accessible from Kalambaka, reachable by train from Athens or Thessaloniki; the town provides accommodation at all price levels and the six accessible monasteries can be covered across two half-days on foot or by bus. Each monastery has its own weekly closing day — no single day of the week is universally open — so plan the sequence in advance. Entrance fees are modest (around EUR 3 per monastery). Dress requirements are strict: women must wear skirts (not trousers) and cover their shoulders; men must wear long trousers. Skirts are available to borrow at some entrances.

Patmos requires a ferry from Piraeus or one of the Dodecanese island-hopping routes; the journey is substantial but the island's combination of the monastery, the Cave of the Apocalypse, and the traditional architecture of the upper town (Chora) justifies a one- or two-night stay. Hosios Loukas is best reached by car from Athens or Delphi — the location in rural Boeotia puts it beyond convenient public transport. Chios and its Nea Moni monastery are accessible by ferry from Piraeus; the monastery is roughly thirty minutes by road from Chios town.
