Armenia adopted Christianity as its state religion in 301 AD, making it the earliest nation to do so. The Armenian Apostolic Church that emerged from that conversion is among the oldest Christian institutions in continuous existence, and the monasteries it built across several centuries of medieval prosperity remain the country's most eloquent monuments. Most were damaged or destroyed during the Mongol invasions of the thirteenth century, the Persian and Ottoman campaigns of later centuries, and the Soviet period, but a remarkable number have been restored and are active today. Armenian monastic architecture developed a distinctive vocabulary — drum-supported conical cupolas, intricate blind arcading, ornate khachkars (carved stone crosses) — that is recognisable across the entire country.

## 1. Tatev Monastery, Syunik

Founded in the ninth century by the Syunik princes and expanded through the fourteenth century, Tatev was for centuries the largest and most important monastery in Armenia, running a university that attracted scholars from across the medieval world. It sits on a basalt promontory above the Vorotan gorge in the far south, connected to the village of Halidzor by the Wings of Tatev reversible aerial tramway (Tatevi Veraprkhik) — at 5.7 kilometres the world's longest non-stop double track reversible cable car when it opened in 2010. The monastery church of St Paul and Peter (895 AD) and the refectory are the principal structures; the compound also contains an unusual oscillating pillar (Gavazan) erected in the ninth century. Tatev is active under the Armenianarchbishopric of Syunik.

## 2. Geghard Monastery, Kotayk

Geghard (the name means "spear," referring to the lance that pierced Christ's side, a relic once kept here) is a UNESCO World Heritage Site set within a gorge of the Azat river fifteen kilometres east of Garni. Part of the monastery complex is carved directly out of the cliff face, including the main chapel (gavit) and two additional rock-cut churches, one with a dome carved from the living rock and decorated with stalactite-form carvings. Founded in the fourth century according to tradition (though current structures date from the twelfth and thirteenth centuries), Geghard is active and draws large numbers of Armenian pilgrims particularly on Sundays and feast days.

## 3. Khor Virap Monastery, Ararat

Khor Virap ("Deep Dungeon") marks the site where St Gregory the Illuminator was imprisoned for thirteen years by King Tiridates III before converting him to Christianity. The monastery sits in the Ararat plain with an unimpeded view of Mount Ararat — a view that has become one of Armenia's defining images, though Ararat itself now lies across the border in Turkey. The dungeon where Gregory was confined is accessible by a narrow iron ladder to a small underground chamber. The monastery is active and serves as a major pilgrimage destination; it is also accessible from Yerevan in under an hour, making it the most visited monastery in the country.

## 4. Noravank Monastery, Vayots Dzor

Noravank ("New Monastery") is the most visually dramatic monastery in Armenia, built in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries in a narrow gorge of red limestone cliffs in Vayots Dzor province. The Church of St John the Baptist (Surb Karapet, 1216) and the two-storey Church of St Astvatsatsin (Holy Mother of God, 1339) sit on natural rock terraces; the upper level of the latter is reached by exterior stone staircases of remarkable narrowness that project without railings from the facade. The monastery was built by the Orbelian princes and contains the khachkars of the sculptor Momik, whose work represents the peak of Armenian stone-carving. The gorge is also notable for its prehistoric rock art.

## 5. Sevanavank, Gegharkunik

Sevanavank ("Monastery of Lake Sevan") occupies a small peninsula — once an island before Soviet-era water extraction lowered the lake level — on the shore of Lake Sevan, Armenia's largest body of water and one of the largest high-altitude lakes in the world. The two surviving churches (of an original three) date from the ninth century. The site is visually striking for its combination of black tuff masonry against the blue water and the surrounding mountain ranges. The monastery is active; its guesthouse accommodates pilgrims and visitors.

## 6. Haghpat Monastery, Lori

Haghpat is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (inscribed together with Sanahin) in the Lori region of northern Armenia. Founded in the tenth century under the Kiurikid kings, it expanded through the thirteenth century into a complex of churches, gavits (assembly halls), a library, a bell tower, and residential buildings. The Cathedral of the Holy Cross (967 AD) is the principal structure; the gavit of Hamazasp (1257) with its carved geometric ceiling is one of the finest interiors in Armenian medieval architecture. The monastery is active under the Diocese of Gugark.

## 7. Sanahin Monastery, Lori

Sanahin ("This is older than that" — a name that refers to its precedence over Haghpat) is a UNESCO World Heritage Site set in the village of the same name in a forested gorge of the Debed river valley. The complex, developed through the tenth to thirteenth centuries, includes two principal churches, three gavits, a library building, and an academy. Sanahin is celebrated as the birthplace of the Soviet aerospace engineers Artem Mikoyan and Anastas Mikoyan, a fact that sits oddly against its medieval splendour but which the local museum emphasises with considerable pride.

## 8. Saghmosavank, Aragatsotn

Saghmosavank ("Monastery of Psalms") was founded in the thirteenth century by Prince Vache Vachutian on a cliff edge above the Kasagh river gorge in Aragatsotn province, with a view toward Mount Aragats. The Church of Zion (1215) and its attached gavit (1225) are well preserved; the site is less visited than Geghard or Noravank, which gives it a quieter, more contemplative atmosphere. The cliff-edge position and the sweep of the gorge below make it one of the most scenically situated monasteries in the country.

## 9. Goshavank, Tavush

Goshavank, in the Dilijan forest region of Tavush province, was founded in 1188 by the Armenian jurist and polymath Mkhitar Gosh, who developed the first Armenian civil code here. The monastery complex includes the principal church, two gavits, a library, a chapel, and numerous carved khachkars including several by Poghos, a student of Momik. The surrounding forest and the cooler northern climate give Goshavank a different character from the sun-baked monasteries of the south.

## 10. Hovhannavank, Aragatsotn

Hovhannavank ("Monastery of St John"), above the Kasagh gorge near Ohanavan village, is one of the earliest surviving monastic complexes in Armenia; the oldest surviving church on the site dates to the fifth century. The twelfth-century Cathedral of St John added a large nave to the earlier structure, and a gavit was added in the thirteenth century by the same Vache Vachutian who built Saghmosavank. The two gorge-edge monasteries are often visited together as a half-day excursion from Yerevan.

## Visiting Armenian Monasteries

Most Armenian monasteries are open daily without charge, though donations are appreciated. Services in the Armenian Apostolic rite use Classical Armenian (Grabar) and follow a distinctive liturgical tradition that differs substantially from both Byzantine and Latin forms. Women are not excluded from any Armenian monastery, but modest dress (covered shoulders and knees) is expected everywhere.
