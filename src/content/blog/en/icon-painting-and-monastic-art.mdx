The icon is not a painting in the Western sense. It is, in Orthodox theology, a window — a material opening onto the divine prototype it depicts. That theological weight shaped every choice a monastic craftsman made: the ratio of linseed oil to pigment, the thickness of the gesso, the number of days a saint's face received without sleep before the gold background was laid. Understanding icon painting means understanding the monastic world that produced it and, in a handful of workshops still operating today, continues to produce it.

## The Russian Schools: Rublev and Theophanes the Greek

The two towering figures of Russian iconography were both connected to the Trinity Lavra of St Sergius near Moscow, and their contrasting approaches still define how practitioners think about the craft. Theophanes the Greek — a Byzantine master who arrived in Novgorod around 1370 — worked with extraordinary speed and emotional intensity. His figures lean into ascetic severity: sunken faces, hard shadows, minimal gold highlight. His surviving frescoes in the Church of the Transfiguration in Novgorod display a painter comfortable improvising at scale, moving without preparatory drawings in a way that astonished his contemporaries.

Andrei Rublev, by contrast, moved with deliberate calm. A monk of the Andronikov Monastery in Moscow and later associated with the Trinity Lavra, Rublev is most celebrated for the Old Testament Trinity icon painted around 1411, now in the Tretyakov Gallery. Where Theophanes dramatised, Rublev resolved. The three angels of the Trinity sit in a circle of mutual attention, their postures creating the geometry of perfect communal stillness. The palette — cool greens, gold, a blue that Russian iconographers call "Rublev blue" — became the benchmark for the Moscow school for the next century. His approach required the painter to pray before working and to treat the board as a liturgical object from the moment the first layer of ground was applied.

## The Cretan School and Mount Athos

The fifteenth and sixteenth centuries produced the Cretan school, a synthesis of late Byzantine refinement and early Italian Renaissance spatial awareness. After the fall of Constantinople in 1453, many Byzantine craftsmen relocated to Venetian-controlled Crete, where they worked simultaneously for Orthodox and Catholic patrons. The resulting style is more plastic than earlier Byzantine icons — figures have volume, drapery has weight — without abandoning the gold ground and the reverse perspective that keep the icon outside ordinary pictorial space.

The most celebrated Cretan master, El Greco (Doménikos Theotokópoulos), trained in this tradition before moving to Venice and Spain. Lesser-known but equally important for the Orthodox world were painters such as Michael Damaskinos and Emmanuel Tzanes, whose panels remain in churches across Crete and the Ionian Islands.

Mount Athos absorbed Cretan workshop methods in the sixteenth century and never fully surrendered them. The workshops of the Holy Mountain — particularly those attached to Vatopedi, Iveron, and Simonopetra — still operate on principles that would be recognisable to a fifteenth-century master. Painters study under senior monastics for years before being permitted to paint a face. The hierarchy is strict: backgrounds first, architecture next, garments, then the nimbus, then hands, then faces last of all. A face is the most sacred surface in the composition and must be approached only after everything else is complete.

## Materials and Technique

Traditional icon painting uses egg tempera on a wooden panel prepared with rabbit-skin gesso. The wood — typically lime, poplar, or cypress — is dried for several years before use. The gesso is built up in many thin layers, each sanded before the next is applied, until the surface is smooth enough to reflect light like ivory. The painter then traces the composition in charcoal, incises the lines lightly into the gesso, and applies a reddish-brown base tone (the sankir) to the areas of flesh.

Highlights are then added not by softening from light to shadow but by building from dark to light — a reversal of Western painting logic that produces the characteristic radiant quality of a well-made icon. Gold leaf, applied to the halo and sometimes the background, is prepared on a ground of Armenian bole, a red clay that gives the gold warmth when it is burnished to a high finish. The entire process for a medium-sized icon takes between two and six weeks, not counting the drying time between sessions.

Gilding with genuine gold leaf requires the gesso ground to be dampened with a breath and the leaf pressed on while the surface is still slightly tacky — a skill that takes months to develop reliable judgement for. Many contemporary workshops use gold powder suspended in gum arabic as a more forgiving alternative, though traditionalists consider the finish inferior.

## Where to Watch Icon Painters at Work

Three places currently offer visitors genuine access to working monastic icon workshops.

The Monastery of the Annunciation at Souroti, near Thessaloniki in Greece, is a women's community that maintains a large icon workshop producing work sold through the monastery shop. Visitors are welcome during the morning visiting hours (generally 9:00–12:00) and the workshop products include small portable icons at modest prices alongside large commissioned pieces.

Optina Pustyn, the historic monastery south of Kaluga in Russia famous for its tradition of elders (startsy), was restored after Soviet closure and now runs an icon workshop where monks produce liturgical items for sale. The monastery is open to visitors daily; the workshop is visible from the gallery of the main church complex.

The Holy Trinity Cathedral at the Schitul Maicilor skete outside Cluj-Napoca in Romania is a smaller community where a Romanian sister trained at an Athonite workshop produces icons in the traditional tempera-on-gesso method. The workshop is not always open to casual visitors, but the convent receives pilgrims and the icons are sold in the small shop by the main gate.

## Recognising the Schools

A practiced eye can distinguish Byzantine regional schools from the palette alone. Greek and Athonite icons favour a warm, amber-tinged gold ground with figures rendered in cool muted greens and blues. Russian icons, particularly the northern Novgorod school, use flatter, more graphic compositions with bold red and ochre tones. The Moscow school softens these contrasts into the gentle, contemplative palette associated with Rublev. Cretan icons are warmer overall, with more naturalistic drapery and a closer integration of Italian spatial conventions.

All schools share the convention of inverse perspective, in which architectural elements open outward toward the viewer rather than receding to a vanishing point. This is not an error or a limitation — it is a deliberate theological statement that the space of the icon opens into the viewer's world rather than withdrawing from it.

## Planning a Visit

If you want to combine monastery visits with attention to icon art, the best concentration of working workshops and historic collections lies in northern Greece (Mount Athos, Thessaloniki's Byzantine Museum), central Russia (the Tretyakov Gallery and Trinity Lavra), and Crete (the Historical Museum in Heraklion). Romania's painted monasteries of Bucovina display exterior fresco programmes of extraordinary scale, a related but distinct tradition.
