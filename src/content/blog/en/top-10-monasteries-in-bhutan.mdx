Bhutan is the last surviving Vajrayana Buddhist kingdom. The state religion — the Drukpa Kagyu school, a branch of Tibetan Buddhism introduced from Tibet in the twelfth century — permeates governance, architecture, daily life, and the calendar to a degree that has no parallel in the modern world. The dzongs (fortress-monasteries) that anchor each district serve simultaneously as administrative headquarters and monastic centres, with both civil servants and monks working within the same whitewashed walls. Access to Bhutan requires a visa and a minimum daily spend (the "Sustainable Development Fee" of USD 100 per night), which limits visitor numbers and preserves an atmosphere unlike anywhere else in Asia.

## 1. Taktsang Palphug Monastery, Paro

Taktsang — the Tiger's Nest — is the most famous image in Bhutan, a cluster of four temples and a hermitage built into a cliff face nine hundred metres above the Paro valley floor. The site is sacred as the place where Guru Rinpoche (Padmasambhava) flew from Tibet on the back of a tigress in the eighth century and meditated in a cave before establishing Buddhism in Bhutan. The current buildings date primarily from the seventeenth century, rebuilt after a fire in 1998. The walk to the monastery takes two to three hours from the road; the upper section involves a descent to a waterfall and a steep climb on stone steps. Photography is not permitted inside the temples. The monastery is active and monks are present year-round.

## 2. Punakha Dzong, Punakha

The administrative and religious centre of Punakha district, built at the confluence of the Pho Chhu and Mo Chhu rivers in 1637 by the Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal (the Tibetan lama who unified Bhutan as a nation). Punakha served as the national capital until 1955, and the central tower (utse) contains the preserved remains of the Zhabdrung. The monastic community of the central monk body, normally headquartered in Thimphu, winters at Punakha from October to May each year, making this the most populated dzong in Bhutan for much of the year. The annual tshechu festival brings the thangka of the Zhabdrung out for public display on a scale matched nowhere else in the country.

## 3. Trongsa Dzong, Trongsa

The largest dzong in Bhutan, Trongsa controls the passes connecting eastern and western Bhutan and was historically the source of the royal family's power — each Crown Prince traditionally serves as Penlop (Governor) of Trongsa before succession. The dzong was built in stages from 1543 and commands a sheer cliff above the Mangde Chhu river. The tower museum (Ta Dzong) adjacent to the main structure houses a collection of royal regalia and religious objects. The position of Trongsa in the centre of the country gives it a strategic importance that the architecture makes immediately legible.

## 4. Tashichho Dzong, Thimphu

The seat of Bhutan's government and the summer residence of the central monk body, Tashichho Dzong in the capital Thimphu was substantially rebuilt in 1962 on the site of an older dzong. The current white-and-red structure houses the offices of the King, the Prime Minister, and several government ministries alongside the monastic wing. The dzong is open to visitors in the afternoon and evening, but the government sections are not accessible. The Thimphu tshechu, held in autumn, is one of the most accessible major festivals in the country.

## 5. Tango Goemba, Thimphu

Founded in the twelfth century and substantially rebuilt by the Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal in the seventeenth, Tango is a Drukpa Kagyu monastery and college (shedra) perched on a forested hilltop above the Thimphu valley. The climb from the road takes about an hour through forest. The monastery is active as a centre of traditional religious studies; the shedra trains monks in the Kagyu scholastic tradition. The forest setting and the mountain views make it worth the walk.

## 6. Cheri Goemba, Thimphu

Cheri (or Chagri) was the first monastery built in Bhutan by the Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal after his arrival from Tibet in 1616, and it served as the residence of the central monk body before the completion of Punakha Dzong. It stands above the Thimphu valley on the opposite bank of the Wang Chhu from Tango Goemba. The monastery houses the reliquary (chorten) of the Zhabdrung's father. Combined with Tango, it forms a half-day or full-day excursion from the capital.

## 7. Gangtey Goenpa, Wangdue Phodrang

The main monastery of the Nyingma school in Bhutan, Gangtey Goenpa sits on a ridge above the Phobjikha valley — a glacial bowl that serves as the winter roosting ground for black-necked cranes arriving from Tibet. The cranes (Grus nigricollis) arrive in late October and depart in February; the valley and the crane festival it generates have become one of the primary wildlife and cultural tourism destinations in the country. The monastery was founded in the seventeenth century by Pema Thinley, a grandson of Pema Lingpa (one of Bhutan's most revered treasure-revealer saints). The current wooden structure dates from a major restoration in 2008.

## 8. Lhuentse Dzong, Lhuentse

The ancestral home of Bhutan's royal family, Lhuentse Dzong sits on a rocky promontory above the Kuri Chhu river in the remote northeast of the country. The dzong is less visited than those in Paro and Punakha, but the journey there — through the narrow Kuri Chhu gorge — is one of the most dramatic road trips in Bhutan. The dzong's inaccessibility has preserved it in a condition that lacks the renovation polish of the western dzongs, giving it an authentic atmosphere that more-visited sites sometimes lack.

## 9. Kurjey Lhakhang, Bumthang

Kurjey ("imprint of the body") takes its name from the imprint of Guru Rinpoche's body that is said to be preserved in the cave behind the earliest of the three temples on the site. The cave itself (inside the first temple, built in 1652) is accessible to visitors. Bumthang district is the spiritual heartland of Bhutan, containing the highest concentration of ancient temples and monasteries in the country, and Kurjey is its most important pilgrimage site. A large juniper tree in the courtyard is said to have grown from Guru Rinpoche's staff.

## 10. Drukgyel Dzong, Paro

Drukgyel ("Victory of Bhutan") was built in 1649 to commemorate Bhutan's defeat of Tibetan invasion attempts. The dzong burned in a fire in 1951 and has not been fully rebuilt, leaving a picturesque ruin at the head of the Paro valley, with Jomolhari (7,326 metres) visible on clear mornings above it. The ruins have been stabilised and are accessible to visitors; they represent a different kind of monastic architecture than the intact dzongs — more fortress than monastery, and visually compelling precisely because of their incomplete state.

## Planning Your Visit

All monasteries and dzongs are on the [map](/map). Bhutan requires entry through a registered tour operator; independent travel is not permitted. Most monastery visits are part of guided itineraries, and guides can navigate the varying protocols around temple access and photography. The major festivals (tshechus) at Paro, Punakha, and Thimphu are the most rewarding times to visit but must be booked months in advance.
