Tibetan Buddhism took its defining form in the seventh and eighth centuries when the Yarlung dynasty kings — Songtsen Gampo, Trisong Detsen, and Tri Ralpachen — invited Indian pandits and Tibetan translators to create a vast corpus of translated Buddhist scriptures and establish the first monasteries. The tradition was nearly extinguished twice: first in the ninth-century persecution under King Langdarma, and again — far more thoroughly — in the Cultural Revolution (1966–1976), when the People's Liberation Army and Red Guard units destroyed an estimated four thousand monasteries across Tibet, killing or imprisoning much of the monastic population. The post-1980 liberalisation allowed limited rebuilding; the current monasteries are partial restorations of their former size, operating under Chinese government oversight that includes limits on monk numbers and mandatory "patriotic education" programmes. This context does not diminish the power of visiting them but must be held in mind.

## 1. Jokhang Temple, Lhasa

The Jokhang is considered the holiest Buddhist site in Tibet, established in the seventh century by King Songtsen Gampo to house the Jowo Shakyamuni — a statue of the Buddha at age twelve brought from China as part of his queen Wencheng's dowry. The statue is considered the most sacred object in Tibetan Buddhism. The Jokhang is surrounded by the Barkhor, a circumambulation circuit that operates continuously with pilgrims prostrating, spinning prayer wheels, and burning incense. The temple's exterior, visible from the Barkhor, is characterised by gilded copper roof structures and the crowd of pilgrims outside regardless of the hour.

## 2. Potala Palace, Lhasa

The Potala — technically a palace rather than a monastery, though it contains chapels, hermitages, and the tombs of eight Dalai Lamas — dominates the Lhasa skyline from its position on Marpo Ri (Red Mountain). Built primarily in the seventeenth century under the Fifth Dalai Lama, it served as the winter residence of the Dalai Lama and the seat of Tibetan government until 1959. The Thirteenth Dalai Lama's tomb chapel is considered particularly sacred. Visitor numbers are now tightly controlled (a daily quota, with timed entry tickets required well in advance) and the pace through the interior is rapid, but the scale and the quality of the contents remain extraordinary.

## 3. Sera Monastery, Lhasa

Sera, one of the three great Gelug monasteries of Lhasa, was founded in 1419 by Jamchen Chojey, a disciple of Tsongkhapa (the founder of the Gelug school). At its peak it housed over five thousand monks; the current population is approximately six hundred. Sera is distinguished from the other great Gelug monasteries by its debate courtyard, where monks engage in formal philosophical debate (tsenshab) every afternoon around 15:00–17:00 — a performance of rigorous scholasticism in which debaters punctuate their questions with sharp hand claps. The debate is open to visitors and is one of the most arresting spectacles in Tibetan monastic life.

## 4. Drepung Monastery, Lhasa

Drepung ("Rice Heap") was the largest monastery in Tibet and, before the Cultural Revolution, the largest in the world — housing over ten thousand monks. Founded in 1416 by Jamyang Chojey, another disciple of Tsongkhapa, Drepung served as the residence of the Dalai Lamas before the Potala was built. The whitewashed complex covers an entire hillside west of Lhasa. The current monk population is approximately seven hundred. The Shoton Festival (Yoghurt Festival) in summer involves the unfurling of a monumental thangka on the hillside above the monastery.

## 5. Ganden Monastery, Lhasa

Ganden ("Joyful"), on Wangbur Mountain forty-seven kilometres from Lhasa, was the first monastery founded by Tsongkhapa himself (1409) and is considered the Gelug motherhouse. It was destroyed with particular thoroughness during the Cultural Revolution — photographs from the 1980s show complete rubble — and has been substantially rebuilt. The Tsonkhapa Lhakhang houses the gilded chörten (reliquary stupa) containing Tsongkhapa's remains. The kora (circumambulation path) around the monastery ridge offers one of the most sweeping landscape views accessible to visitors near Lhasa.

## 6. Tashilhunpo Monastery, Shigatse

Tashilhunpo in Shigatse, Tibet's second city, was founded in 1447 by Gedun Drup (posthumously designated the First Dalai Lama) and became the seat of the Panchen Lama — the second most important figure in Gelug Buddhism. The largest functioning monastery in Tibet, Tashilhunpo houses over six hundred monks and contains the gilded chapel of the Eleventh Panchen Lama (the candidate recognised by the Chinese government, whose legitimacy is disputed by the Tibetan diaspora). The monastery's white-walled compound and golden roofs are visible from across Shigatse.

## 7. Samye Monastery, Shannan

Samye, the oldest monastery in Tibet, was founded in 779 under King Trisong Detsen, who invited the Indian master Padmasambhava (Guru Rinpoche) and the abbot Shantarakshita to establish the first community of Tibetan monks. The monastery's plan replicates the Buddhist cosmological model of Mount Meru and the four continents; the central temple is surrounded by smaller temples at the cardinal and intercardinal directions within a circular wall. Samye is associated with the Nyingma school — the oldest school of Tibetan Buddhism — and is considered the founding site of the entire Tibetan monastic tradition. Access requires a ferry across the Brahmaputra.

## 8. Mindrolling Monastery, Shannan

Mindrolling ("Place of Perfect Emancipation") was founded in 1676 by Terdak Lingpa, a treasure-revealer (terton) of the Nyingma school, and became one of the six mother monasteries of the Nyingma tradition. Destroyed in the Cultural Revolution, it has been partially rebuilt. The Mindrolling tradition has been particularly active in preserving and transmitting Nyingma arts, including thangka painting and sand mandala construction. A branch Mindrolling monastery was established in Dehradun, India by the Mindrolling Trichen who went into exile in 1959.

## 9. Ralung Monastery, Shigatse

Ralung, in the Nyang Chu valley of Gyantse district, was founded in 1180 by Tsangpa Gyare, the founder of the Drukpa Kagyu school — the school that subsequently became the state religion of Bhutan. Ralung is therefore the origin of Bhutan's dzong tradition and the Drukpa Kagyu lineage that governs it. The monastery complex includes the original medieval temple alongside later additions; it is less visited than the Lhasa monasteries and offers a more direct encounter with a living rural monastic community.

## 10. Drigung Monastery, Lhasa

Drigung Til, northeast of Lhasa in the Drigung valley, is the head monastery of the Drigung Kagyu school, founded in 1179 by Jigten Sumgon. The monastery was historically associated with sky burial (jhator), and the Drigung sky burial site nearby remains one of the few in Tibet where the practice is still performed. The setting — a remote valley with the monastery climbing the hillside — is dramatic; the distance from Lhasa (approximately 150 kilometres by road) keeps visitor numbers modest.

## Visiting Tibet

All foreign visitors require a Tibet Travel Permit in addition to a Chinese visa; permits must be arranged through a registered travel agency and are not available independently. Group travel with a registered guide is required at all times. Altitude sickness is a genuine risk — Lhasa sits at 3,650 metres, Ganden at 4,300 metres.
