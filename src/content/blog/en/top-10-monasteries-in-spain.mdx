The Spanish monarchy's relationship with monasticism was one of the most intimate in European history. Kings endowed monasteries as burial pantheons, retired to convents after abdication, and used monastic institutions as instruments of cultural prestige and political consolidation. The result is a series of royal foundations — El Escorial, Las Huelgas, Guadalupe, Yuste — in which the quality of architecture, art, and archive is of the highest order. Alongside these royal foundations, Spain's medieval monastic reform movements produced communities — Poblet, Santes Creus, the Riojan monasteries of San Millán — that are essential to the story of how Spanish and Castilian culture took its early form.

## 1. El Escorial, Madrid

The Real Monasterio de San Lorenzo de El Escorial, a UNESCO World Heritage Site in the Sierra de Guadarrama northwest of Madrid, was built by Philip II between 1563 and 1584 as a monastery, royal palace, mausoleum, library, and seminary combined in a single structure of monumental scale and deliberate austerity. The architect Juan de Herrera's austere grid of grey granite — the Herreran style — was the dominant influence on Spanish official architecture for a century. The Pantheon of Kings beneath the high altar holds the remains of nearly all Spanish monarchs from Charles I onward. The Hieronymite monastery at the centre of the complex is still active. The library's collection of manuscripts and early printed books is one of the finest in Spain.

## 2. Montserrat, Barcelona

The Benedictine Abbey of Santa Maria de Montserrat, built at the base of the extraordinary serrated mountain massif northwest of Barcelona, is the principal pilgrimage destination of Catalonia. The Black Madonna (La Moreneta), a twelfth-century Romanesque polychrome statue, is the focus of pilgrimage that has continued without interruption through the Spanish Civil War (when the community was scattered) and beyond. The current Benedictine community of approximately seventy monks runs a choir school (the Escolania, one of Europe's oldest boys' choirs), a publishing house, a museum, and guesthouses for thousands of visitors weekly.

## 3. Monasterio de Yuste, Extremadura

Yuste, in the Extremaduran hills, is a Hieronymite monastery where the Holy Roman Emperor Charles V retired in 1556 after his abdication, spending his final two years in a modest suite built adjacent to the monastery church so he could hear Mass from his bed. Charles died here in 1558; his body was transferred to El Escorial, but the rooms he occupied — preserved with care — convey the remarkable intimacy of the most powerful man in Europe living in voluntary poverty next to a small community of friars.

## 4. Poblet Monastery, Tarragona

Poblet, a UNESCO World Heritage Site in the Conca de Barberà wine region of Catalonia, is the largest inhabited Cistercian monastery in the world and the burial church of the Crown of Aragon. Founded in 1150 by Ramon Berenguer IV, it reached its peak in the fourteenth century when the Aragonese kings made it the royal pantheon. The monastery was sacked in 1835 and the royal tombs destroyed; the tombs were later restored and the royal remains returned. A Cistercian community of approximately fifty monks was established in 1940 and continues today.

## 5. Santes Creus Monastery, Tarragona

The second great Cistercian monastery of Catalonia, Santes Creus, is now a museum (the community left in 1835) rather than an active monastery, but preserves the most complete medieval Cistercian complex in Spain, including the original chapter house, dormitory, royal palace, and Gothic cloister. The tombs of Kings Peter III and James II of Aragon are in the church. The combination of royal patronage and Cistercian austerity produced a building of striking spatial quality.

## 6. Guadalupe Monastery, Extremadura

The Real Monasterio de Santa María de Guadalupe, a UNESCO World Heritage Site in the Extremaduran mountains, holds one of the most venerated images in Spain — the Black Madonna of Guadalupe, a fourteenth-century wooden statue to which both Christopher Columbus and Hernán Cortés attributed their success. The monastery developed from the fourteenth century into a large complex combining Gothic, Mudéjar, and Baroque architecture; the sacristy contains paintings by Zurbarán (eight scenes from the life of St Jerome) of the highest quality. The monastery is active under the Franciscan order.

## 7. San Millán de la Cogolla, La Rioja

The twin monasteries of San Millán — Suso (the upper, sixth-century cave church and monastery) and Yuso (the lower, rebuilt in the sixteenth century as a royal monastery) — are a UNESCO World Heritage Site in the Cárdenas valley of La Rioja. The site is celebrated as the birthplace of written Castilian Spanish: the earliest known glosses in the vernacular Castilian language were written in the margins of a Latin manuscript at Suso in the tenth or eleventh century. The Augustinian community at Yuso still occupies the lower monastery.

## 8. Las Huelgas Burgos, Castile

The Real Monasterio de Santa María la Real de Las Huelgas in Burgos is a Cistercian convent founded in 1187 by King Alfonso VIII of Castile and his queen Eleanor, daughter of Henry II of England and Eleanor of Aquitaine. It served as the burial pantheon of the Castilian royal house — over thirty medieval royal and noble tombs are preserved — and as a uniquely powerful institution: the abbess of Las Huelgas held quasi-episcopal authority over a network of dependent monasteries, a power that was gradually curtailed by the papacy but not extinguished until the nineteenth century. The convent is still active; visits are by guided tour.

## 9. Santo Domingo de Silos, Castile

Silos, a Benedictine monastery in a village in the Castilian meseta south of Burgos, became internationally famous in 1994 when its recording of Gregorian chant reached the top of the classical music charts. The cloister (eleventh and twelfth centuries) is one of the masterpieces of Romanesque sculpture in Spain, its corner piers bearing narrative reliefs of the life of Christ and its capitals decorated with interlaced and zoomorphic ornament of exceptional quality. The Benedictine community is active and the monks still sing the full office; the Sunday and feast day Masses attract visitors specifically for the chant.

## 10. Vallbona de les Monges, Lleida

Vallbona de les Monges ("Vallbona of the Nuns") in the Catalan inland, founded in the twelfth century, is the third of Catalonia's great Cistercian royal monasteries alongside Poblet and Santes Creus. It is uniquely a women's community and has remained continuously occupied by Cistercian nuns since its foundation — one of the few medieval Spanish convents never suppressed. The church contains the tombs of queens of Aragon; the Romanesque and Gothic cloister is the finest architectural feature.

## Planning Spanish Monastery Visits

El Escorial is accessible by train from Madrid (one hour). Montserrat requires a rack railway from Monistrol. Poblet, Santes Creus, and Vallbona form a Cistercian triangle in Catalonia best explored by car.
