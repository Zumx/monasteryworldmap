The Athos peninsula, a narrow finger of land extending forty-six kilometres into the Aegean from the three-pronged Chalkidiki coastline, is unlike any other place on earth. Legally part of Greece, it is governed as an autonomous monastic state — the Holy Community (Agía Koinótis) — under the spiritual authority of the Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople. Twenty ruling monasteries share governance of the peninsula, each represented in the governing assembly by its abbot or a delegate. No woman has entered the territory legally since 1060, when the emperor Constantine IX Monomachos issued the first formal exclusion. The peninsula's monks today number somewhere between two thousand and two thousand five hundred, the highest the Holy Mountain has seen since the nineteenth century.

## The Twenty Ruling Monasteries

Each of the twenty monasteries is sovereign within its own territory, meaning it controls its guesthouses, its church calendar, its agricultural land, and its relationship with visitors independently. They are listed here in their traditional order of precedence within the Holy Community.

The Great Lavra (Megisti Lavra), founded in 963 by St Athanasius the Athonite with the support of Emperor Nikephoros II Phokas, is the oldest and most senior monastery on the Mountain. Its library contains over two thousand manuscripts. Vatopedi, second in precedence, is a vast cenobitic community in a natural bay on the northeastern coast; its treasury includes the Belt of the Virgin and a collection of Byzantine relics, jewellery, and documents of outstanding historical significance. Iveron, a Georgian foundation of the tenth century, houses the icon of the Portaitissa (Keeper of the Gate), one of the most venerated icons in the Orthodox world. Hilandar is the Serbian national monastery, founded in 1198 by the future St Sava and his father Stefan Nemanja; it has been rebuilt after fires but remains a living monument to Serbian Orthodox culture. Dionysiou, founded in the fourteenth century, occupies a dramatic position on a cliff above the southwestern shore.

Koutloumousiou and Pantokrator are mid-peninsula communities; Xeropotamou, despite its modest size, holds one of the largest relics of the True Cross in existence. Zograf is the Bulgarian national monastery, founded in the tenth century and rebuilt substantially in the nineteenth. Dochiariou, Karakallou, and Filotheou occupy the inland and eastern portions of the peninsula. Simonopetra — perhaps the most photogenic monastery on the Mountain, a multi-storey tower of wooden balconies rising from a sheer cliff — is a Greek cenobitic community restored under the influential Elder Aimilianos of Simonopetra in the 1970s. Agiou Pavlou (St Paul's) is situated at the southwestern tip near the peak. Stavronikita, the smallest of the twenty ruling monasteries, was restored in the 1960s under the direction of the architect Aris Konstantinidis. Xenophontos, near the landing port of Daphni, is one of the more accessible. Grigoriou, on the southwestern coast, is a well-preserved cenobitic community of medium size.

Esphigmenou, near Vatopedi on the northern coast, has been in canonical dispute with the Ecumenical Patriarchate since the 1970s over the question of ecumenical dialogue; its monks fly a black flag from the walls and consider themselves in a state of spiritual resistance. The monastery remains in physical occupation by its own community despite ongoing legal proceedings. The Russian house, St Panteleimonos (Russikon), was at its peak in the early twentieth century the largest monastery on the Mountain with over a thousand monks; it declined during the Soviet period but has been recovering since the 1990s under increased Russian interest in the Holy Mountain. Konstamonitou, the smallest and most modest of the twenty, holds last precedence in the assembly.

## The Abaton: No Women Allowed

The exclusion of women from Athos — the abaton — is the most discussed and most controversial aspect of the Holy Mountain. The ban was first formalised under the imperial chrysobull of 1046 and has been maintained without interruption since 1060. It applies to women of any religion, nationality, or purpose; no exception has been recognised by the Holy Community in modern times. The European Parliament has passed multiple resolutions calling for the ban's removal on the grounds that it violates EU law on equal treatment; Greece maintains that Athos's special status under the Treaty of Lausanne and its subsequent EU accession protocol exempts the peninsula from standard equal-access requirements.

Practically, the abaton is enforced at sea: the ferry from Ouranoupoli stops at a defined exclusion zone, and coast guard vessels patrol the waters. Female scholars seeking access to Athonite manuscripts must work through the monastery's own microfilming and digitisation programmes.

## The Diamonitirion Permit

Access to Athos for male visitors is controlled by the Diamonitirion, an entry permit issued by the Civil Governor of Mount Athos in Thessaloniki and by the Athos Pilgrims' Bureau in Ouranoupoli. Orthodox Christian men receive a permit valid for four days (extendable within the Mountain), with a daily quota of one hundred. Non-Orthodox men receive a permit valid for the same duration under a separate quota of ten per day. Applications should be made at least two to three months ahead for peak season (April–October) as places fill quickly.

The permit is collected in person at Ouranoupoli, where the ferry departs. The ferry lands at Daphni (the main port) or at Iveron depending on the day's schedule. From Daphni, a bus connects to the administrative capital, Karyes, from which visitors walk or take minibuses to individual monasteries. Most monasteries offer guesthouse accommodation for a night; guests receive dinner and breakfast as part of the hospitality, and a small donation (typically EUR 10–15 per night) is customary. Conditions vary substantially between monasteries — some offer comfortable private rooms, others dormitory bunks.

## Russian, Serbian, and Bulgarian Houses

Three of the twenty ruling monasteries carry the identity of Slavic national churches, a legacy of the medieval period when Byzantine-affiliated rulers competed to endow prestige foundations on the Holy Mountain. Hilandar (Serbian), Zograf (Bulgarian), and Panteleimonos (Russian) each maintain their own language of worship alongside Greek and Church Slavonic. Visiting Orthodox Christians from those countries may have a particular emotional connection to their national house, but all three accept visitors of any nationality.

The Russian presence on Athos has fluctuated with political conditions. In the early 2000s, Panteleimonos had fewer than fifty aging monks; by 2015, the number had risen above sixty and a substantial building restoration programme was underway, partly funded by the Russian state. The relationship between the Russian state's cultural support for Athos and the monastery's canonical independence from it is a recurring subject of discussion within the Holy Community.

## Practical Matters

The ferry from Ouranoupoli departs daily in the morning. The crossing to Daphni takes approximately two hours. No currency card payments are accepted on the peninsula; bring cash in euros. Mobile signal exists in some areas but internet access is restricted in guesthouses. The calendar on Athos follows the Julian Old Style, which runs thirteen days behind the Gregorian calendar used in Greece and the rest of the world. Feast days and fasting periods follow the Byzantine typikon and differ from the calendar most Western visitors are accustomed to.
