Japanese Buddhism arrived from China and Korea in the sixth century and developed over the following fifteen centuries into a remarkable range of schools, each with its own monastic culture, architectural tradition, and practice emphasis. The great temple complexes (jiin) that house monastic communities in Japan are typically compounds of multiple buildings — the main hall (hondo or hatto), meditation hall (zendo), monks' quarters (sodo), dharma hall for teachings, and sometimes an extensive system of sub-temples, each with its own abbot and monastic population. The ten communities listed here represent the major Japanese traditions and together provide a survey of the country's exceptional Buddhist heritage.

## 1. Eihei-ji, Fukui

Eihei-ji ("Temple of Eternal Peace") is the head training monastery of the Soto Zen school, founded in 1244 by Dogen Zenji, the philosopher who brought Soto Zen from China and defined its practice around shikantaza ("just sitting"). The monastery in a cedar forest in Fukui Prefecture receives approximately two hundred training monks at any given time, following a schedule in which the day begins before 4:00 and every activity — eating, cleaning, washing — is performed as practice. Day visitors are welcome and can walk through the seventy-building complex on wooden covered walkways; overnight stays in the shukubo are available for advance booking. The morning ceremonies, in which monks move through the complex in robes for the first service of the day, are among the most visually compelling monastic rituals in Japan.

## 2. Soji-ji, Yokohama

The second head monastery of the Soto Zen school (co-equal with Eihei-ji in the school's administration), Soji-ji moved from its original location in Ishikawa Prefecture to Tsurumi in Yokohama in 1911 after a fire. The current complex is large and partly modern in character, but the scale of its monastic training programme — accepting monks from across Japan and internationally — makes it the more accessible of the two Soto head temples. The cemetery contains the graves of numerous important Soto figures.

## 3. Daitoku-ji, Kyoto

Daitoku-ji is a Rinzai Zen monastery complex in the north of Kyoto, founded in 1319, that contains within its grounds twenty-four sub-temples, of which approximately twelve are open to visitors at any given time. Each sub-temple has its own abbot, its own garden, and its own collection of art. The dry gardens (karesansui) of Daitoku-ji sub-temples — particularly Daisen-in (1513) and Ryogen-in — are among the most accomplished rock garden compositions in Japan. The main monastery is still active as a training temple; public access is restricted to the sub-temples.

## 4. Myoshin-ji, Kyoto

The largest Rinzai Zen monastery complex in Japan, Myoshin-ji in western Kyoto was founded in 1342 on the site of a retired emperor's villa. The compound contains forty-six sub-temples within its walls and covers an area large enough to be navigated by a distinctive internal road system. The main monastery buildings — the hatto (dharma hall) with its famous ceiling painting of a dragon, the bath house, and the meditation hall — are accessible on guided tours. The sub-temples around the perimeter take guests in shukubo accommodation; several offer zazen meditation sessions for visitors.

## 5. Kongobu-ji, Mount Koya (Koyasan)

Mount Koya (Koyasan) in Wakayama Prefecture is the headquarters of Shingon Buddhism, founded in 816 by Kukai (Kobo Daishi), who introduced esoteric Vajrayana Buddhism to Japan from Tang dynasty China. The mountain community comprises over a hundred temples, the most important of which is Kongobu-ji, the formal head temple. The mountain's defining feature is the forest cemetery of Okunoin, extending for two kilometres beneath ancient Japanese cedar (sugi) trees and containing thousands of stone lanterns, grave stupas, and the mausoleum of Kobo Daishi himself — considered by Shingon Buddhists to be meditating rather than dead, receiving food offerings daily. Approximately fifty temples offer shukubo accommodation.

## 6. Enryaku-ji, Mount Hiei

Enryaku-ji on Mount Hiei northeast of Kyoto is the head temple of the Tendai school, founded in 788 by Saicho (Dengyo Daishi). The mountain complex — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — extends across three distinct areas (Todo, Saito, and Yokawa) connected by forest roads. The Tendai tradition on Mount Hiei produced many of the founding figures of later Japanese Buddhism: Honen (Pure Land), Shinran (Jodo Shinshu), Dogen (Soto Zen), Eisai (Rinzai Zen), and Nichiren all trained here before founding their own schools. The kaihogyo — the thousand-day circumambulation practice requiring practitioners to walk a mountain marathon equivalent daily for up to seven years — originated here and is still performed by a small number of Tendai monks.

## 7. Todai-ji, Nara

Todai-ji, the head temple of the Kegon school, was founded in 743 by Emperor Shomu as the central temple of a national network of provincial temples. The Great Buddha Hall (Daibutsuden) houses a bronze Buddha (Vairocana, 15 metres high) that is the largest bronze statue in the world, cast in 752. The hall itself, though rebuilt at two-thirds of its original size after fires, remains the largest wooden structure in the world. The deer that roam freely through the Nara Park surrounding the temple are considered sacred messengers. Todai-ji is active as a temple and receives millions of visitors annually.

## 8. Horyu-ji, Nara

Horyu-ji, near Nara, was founded in 607 by Prince Shotoku and contains the oldest surviving wooden structures in the world — the Western Precinct's five-storied pagoda and kondo (Golden Hall) date from around 670–700 AD. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, the temple belongs to the Hosso school of Japanese Buddhism and is active. The Yumedono (Hall of Dreams), in the Eastern Precinct, houses the Guze Kannon — a seventh-century camphor wood statue revealed to the public only twice yearly — which is one of the most significant early sculptures in Japan.

## 9. Engaku-ji, Kamakura

Engaku-ji, founded in 1282 by the Zen master Mugaku Sogen at the invitation of the Kamakura regent Hojo Tokimune following the defeat of the Mongol invasions, is a major Rinzai Zen training monastery at the northern edge of Kamakura. The monastery grounds contain the Shariden — a Chinese Song dynasty-style building housing a relic of the Buddha — that is the oldest Zen building in Japan. Engaku-ji produced a series of significant twentieth-century teachers, including Shaku Soen (who took Zen to the West at the 1893 Parliament of Religions) and D.T. Suzuki. Zazen meditation sessions for visitors are available on weekend mornings.

## 10. Ryoan-ji, Kyoto

Ryoan-ji is a Rinzai Zen temple best known for its rock garden — fifteen stones in white gravel, designed in the late fifteenth century, which has become perhaps the most famous single garden composition in the world. The garden's principle of incompleteness (only fourteen stones are visible from any one viewpoint) is considered an expression of Zen insight. The temple is active; a large pond garden surrounds the main complex and the Kyoto UNESCO World Heritage designation covers the site. Early morning visits before the main tourist influx preserve something of the intended atmosphere.

## Planning Japanese Temple Visits

Kyoto and Nara provide the densest concentration; Mount Koya requires a full day from Osaka (cable car and mountain rail). Eihei-ji is best reached by direct bus from Fukui. Most temples charge a modest entrance fee (500–1,000 yen). Dress modestly, remove footwear when directed, and observe silence in meditation halls.
